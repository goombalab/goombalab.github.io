Since the release of Mamba-2 in mid-2024, we've seen the majority of new hybrid models and linear works build with Mamba-2 instead of Mamba-2, for good reason as well!
Mamba-2 correctly identified that training efficiency was paramount for adoption and simplified the underlying SSM mechanism to enable $2-8\times$ training boosts compared to its predecessor.

Since then, while there still is a lot of focus towards pretraining, the general zeitgeist has shifted to inference.
The scaling of post-training, especially with reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) for coding or mathematics require large amounts of model generations, aka rollouts, and most recently, agentic workflows, such as Codex, Claude Code, or even ~~Clawdbot~~ OpenClaw, have demonstrated the widespread demand in deploying models across various stacks.

Despite the clear, growing importance of of inference, many linear architectures, including Mamba-2, were developed from a training-first perspective, Mamba-3, instead, approaches architecture design from an inference-first perspective. 

## Mamba-3 Model

With the new age of inference, models should be developed to advance the Pareto frontier of model quality and inference efficiency.
The main attraction of linear models is found within their name --- their linearly-scaling compute requirements (in relation to sequenece length) associated with a fixed state size.

Unfortunately, the memory and compute strength of linear models is also a factor for their weakness.
A fixed state size, as opposed to a growing KV cache, coupled with state transition simplifications to improve training efficiency can hamper the expressivity and abilities of such models.
Futhermore, the constant compute associated with each recurrent step during inference leaves lots of compute idle throughout the memory-bound decode process.

With these issues in mind, we improve upon Mamba-2 in core ways that:
1. increase the expressivity of the SSM mechanism through a more general recurrence derived from first priciples
2. expand the state-tracking capabilities of the underlying mechanism by modeling a complex system without adjusting the underlying implementation through a clever trick, and
3. improve general performance of the model with little impact on decode latency by using mult-input, multi-output (MIMO) SSMs instead of current single-input, single-output (SISO) SSMs.

While maintaining the same state size as prior work, Mamba-3 pushes the frontier of performance while maintaining the same state size as prior work. 

Notably, all three of our methodological improvements are inspired from the more canonical control theory and state space model toolbox (we have seen some control theory people upset that "SSM" now generally refers to the structured state space models used in deep learning --- sorry!)
While most recent work fall under the linear attention or test-time training framework, we hope that our principled changes demonstrate that there is a lot more to be discovered (or rediscovered) from the good ole fundamentals.

## Architecture

We've revamped the architecture a bit to make things smoother and more in-line with standard models.

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/2026-02-10-mamba-3/mamba3-arch.png" title="Mamba-3 Architecture" caption="Mamba-3 Architecture" %}

We added in QKNorm (or BCNorm in this case), which we found to also empirically stabilize training of pure Mamba-3 models.
The RMSNorm introduced in Mamba-2 is rendered optional with the new norm, but we find that it should probably be kept for hybrid models due to length extrapolation capabilties; more on this later!

We were also able to get rid of the pesky short convolution with a combination of biases on B and C and our new discretization-based recurrence (which itself is implicit conv, refer to the second part of our blog for more on this).
The short causal convolution, now used heavily by the majority of linear models <d-cite key='gu2024mambalineartimesequencemodeling'></d-cite><d-cite key='dao2024transformersssmsgeneralizedmodels'></d-cite><d-cite key='yang2025gateddeltanetworksimproving'></d-cite><d-cite key='sun2025learninglearntesttime'></d-cite>, was first popularized by Mamba-1, which used it as a simpler version of the shift SSM from H3 <d-cite key='fu2023hungryhungryhipposlanguage'></d-cite>, itself inspired by the "smeared" induction heads work by Anthropic <d-cite key='olsson2022context'></d-cite>.
{% details How/why can the short conv be replaced? %}
Ultimately, we're not exactly sure!

We have a couple of hypothesis of what this might be happening, some more mechanistic than others.
One is that the additional biases upweights the "attention" applied to the most recent tokens, which is similar to the mechanistic merging of tokens done by a short conv.

Based on the SSD framework proposed in Mamba-2, the output of an SSM depends on the summed dot products between the current $C$ and all previous $B$'s with increasing decay the further back.
The addition of biases applied to both $B, C$ would mean that the dot product would be larger on average and may mitigate the decay to some degree for the nearest tokens.
