We introduced our Mamba-3 model in [Part I]({% post_url 2026-02-10-mamba3-part1 %}) in which we mentioned that the three core methodological changes were inspired from the SSM perspective.
Here, we'll actually do a deep dive what each of these three improvements entail and how we derived them.

But first, let’s recap the underlying state space model and its background.

##  State Space Foundations
The state space model, at its most primative, is a simple, continuous ordinary differential equation (ODE).
The input $x(t) \in \mathbb{R}$ is mapped to output $y(t) \in \mathbb{R}$ through a hidden state $h(t) \in \mathbb{R}^N$, where $N$ is the state size.
In past, in both deep learning and classical control theory, these systems were linear time-invariant (LTI), where the "state decay" transition $A \in \mathbb{R}^{(N\times N)}$, input modulating $B \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$, and output modulating $C \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ terms were constant.

$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(t) &= A h(t) + B x(t) \\
y(t) &= C^\top h(t)
\end{aligned}
$$

Upon discretization with one's favorite method, e.g., zero-order hold (ZOH), a familiar recurrence would arise, like the one referenced in Mamba-1.

$$
\begin{aligned}
h_{t} &= e^{\Delta_t A_t} h_{t-1}+ A_t^{-1}(e^{\Delta_t A_t} - I)\Delta_t\,B_t\,x_t \\
y_t &= C_t^\top h_t
\end{aligned}
$$

where the discretized A and B are now $e^{\Delta_t A_t}$ and $A_t^{-1}(e^{\Delta_t A_t} - I)\Delta_t\,B_t$ respectively.

Eagle-eyed readers may ask "how do you go from a LTI system to a linear-time varying (LTV) one?" --- if you did, good job; the answer is revealed below!

We’ll let you in on a little secret: prior Mamba discretizations use a heuristic and just made the fixed variables A, B, and C time-varying!


No worries if this feels uneasy.

This recurrence can be applied in a multitude of ways to sequence modeling, which maps an input $X \in \mathbb{R}^{(T,P)} \to Y \in \mathbb{R}^{(T,P)}$.
In practice, due to computational constraints, the transition matrix $A$ is structured, e.g., diagonal or scalar times identity.

Great, now we’ve set up the underlying mechanism of Mamba used in both Mamba-1 and Mamba-2!

### The Past: Mamba-1
Mamba-1 took the above recurrence and applied it independently to each dimension of an input.
Each dimension of input $X$ had its own SSM which was modulated by its unique diagonal $A$ transition.

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/2026-02-10-mamba-3/mamba1-vs-mamba2.png" title="Mamba-1 vs Mamba-2" caption="Recurrent view of Mamba-1 vs Mamba-2. Mamba-1's transition is diagonal while Mamba-2's is scalar times identity. Image adapted from <d-cite key='gu2022parameterizationinitializationdiagonalstate'></d-cite> by Nano🍌." %}

The large $P\times N$-sized state of Mamba-1 was the driver of its competitive performance against the Transformer, and its hardware-aware algorithm enabled the feasible computation at such a large state size, something that prior works lacked<d-cite key='gu2022efficientlymodelinglongsequences'></d-cite><d-cite key='smith2023simplifiedstatespacelayers'></d-cite>. 
Given its strong modeling capabilities, Mamba-1 really kicked off the flavor of research that many linear models we see today follow.

But calculating the recurrence is hard, expensive, and most importantly, doesn't use many matmuls our expensive GPUs are built for.
Mamba-1’s associative scan didn’t fully utilize those tensor cores, leaving money on the table (or on the rack in this case)! 

### The Present: Mamba-2
Mamba-2 realized that if the transition matrix were further simplified (scalar times identity instead of diagonal), the recurrence could be unrolled and represented in its "parallel form."
This form itself could be then calculated with matrix multiplications!
Along with some architectural modifications, e.g., sharing of $B, C$ within groups of SSMs, analogous to the heads of attention, Mamba-2 used an efficient chunk-based algorithm that sped up training by $2-8\times$.

That’s about it for our rapid rundown.

## The Future: Mamba-3
Three core improvements introduced in Mamba-3 are rooted in classical state-space theory:
1. Improving underlying ODE discretization to more generalized recurrence
2. Converting to complex-valued SSM without engineering challenges of imaginary numbers
3. Increasing expressivity without state size increases with multi-input, multi-output (MIMO)

Let's get started with our first one, better discretization.
