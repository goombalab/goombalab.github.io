We introduced our Mamba-3 model in [Part I]({% post_url 2026-02-10-mamba3-part1 %}) in which we mentioned that the three core methodological changes were inspired from the SSM perspective.
Here, we'll actually do a deep dive into what each of these three improvements entail and how we derived them.

But first, let’s recap the underlying state space model and its background.

##  State Space Foundations
The state space model, at its most primitive, is a simple, continuous ordinary differential equation (ODE).
The input $x(t) \in \mathbb{R}$ is mapped to output $y(t) \in \mathbb{R}$ through a hidden state $h(t) \in \mathbb{R}^N$, where $N$ is the state size.
In past, in both deep learning and classical control theory, these systems were linear time-invariant (LTI), where the "state decay" transition $A \in \mathbb{R}^{(N\times N)}$, input modulating $B \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$, and output modulating $C \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ terms were constant.

$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(t) &= A h(t) + B x(t) \\
y(t) &= C^\top h(t)
\end{aligned}
$$

Upon discretization with one's favorite method, e.g., zero-order hold (ZOH), a familiar recurrence would arise, like the one referenced in Mamba-1.

$$
\begin{aligned}
h_{t} &= e^{\Delta_t A_t} h_{t-1}+ A_t^{-1}(e^{\Delta_t A_t} - I)\Delta_t\,B_t\,x_t \\
y_t &= C_t^\top h_t
\end{aligned}
$$

where the discretized $\bar{A}$ and $\bar{B}$ are now $e^{\Delta_t A_t}$ and $A_t^{-1}(e^{\Delta_t A_t} - I)\Delta_t\,B_t$ respectively.

Eagle-eyed readers may ask "how does one go from a LTI system to a linear-time varying (LTV) system?" --- if you did, good job; the answer is revealed below!
{% details Aside on prior Mamba discretizations %}
We’ll let you in on a little secret: prior Mamba discretizations use a heuristic and just made the fixed variables A, B, and C time-varying!

No worries if this feels uneasy.
We also felt so which is why we formalized the discretization later.
{% enddetails %}

This recurrence can be applied in a multitude of ways to sequence modeling, which maps an input $X \in \mathbb{R}^{(T,P)} \to Y \in \mathbb{R}^{(T,P)}$.
In practice, due to computational constraints, the transition matrix $A$ is structured, e.g., diagonal or scalar times identity.

Great, now we’ve set up the underlying mechanism of Mamba used in both Mamba-1 and Mamba-2!

### The Past: Mamba-1
Mamba-1 took the above recurrence and applied it independently to each dimension of an input.
Each dimension of input $X$ had its own SSM which was modulated by its unique diagonal $A$ transition.

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/2026-02-10-mamba-3/mamba1-vs-mamba2-v2.png" title="Mamba-1 vs Mamba-2" caption="Recurrent view of Mamba-1 vs Mamba-2. Mamba-1's transition is diagonal while Mamba-2's is scalar times identity. Image adapted from <d-cite key='gu2022parameterizationinitializationdiagonalstate'></d-cite> by Nano🍌." %}

The large $P\times N$-sized state of Mamba-1 was the driver of its competitive performance against the Transformer, and its hardware-aware algorithm enabled the feasible computation at such a large state size, something that prior works lacked<d-cite key='gu2022efficientlymodelinglongsequences'></d-cite><d-cite key='smith2023simplifiedstatespacelayers'></d-cite>. 
Given its strong modeling capabilities, Mamba-1 really kicked off the flavor of research that many linear models we see today follow.

But calculating the recurrence is hard, expensive, and most importantly, doesn't use many matrix multiplications (matmuls) our expensive GPUs are built for.
Mamba-1’s associative scan didn’t fully utilize those tensor cores, leaving money on the table (or on the rack in this case)! 

### The Present: Mamba-2
Mamba-2 realized that if the transition matrix were further simplified (scalar times identity instead of diagonal), the recurrence could be unrolled and represented in its "parallel form."
This form itself could be then calculated with matrix multiplications!
Along with some architectural modifications, e.g., sharing of $B, C$ within groups of SSMs, analogous to the heads of attention, Mamba-2 used an efficient chunk-based algorithm that sped up training by $2-8\times$.

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/2024-05-31-mamba-2/semiseparable.png" title="Parallel View of Mamba-2" caption="The Mamba-2 recurrence can be expanded into a matrix form that enables an efficient matmul based algorithm." %}

That’s about it for our rapid rundown.
If any of this sounds interesting and you haven’t read the original work, please do here: [Mamba-1 Paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.00752), [Mamba-2 Paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.21060), [Mamba-2 Blog]({% post_url 2024-05-31-mamba2-part1-model %}).

## The Future: Mamba-3
Three core improvements introduced in Mamba-3 are rooted in classical state-space theory:
1. Improving underlying ODE discretization to more generalized recurrence
2. Converting to complex-valued SSM without engineering challenges of imaginary numbers
3. Increasing expressivity without state size increases with multi-input, multi-output (MIMO)

Let's jump straight into it.

### Formalizing Discretization
Okay, let’s actually figure out a way to discretize our LTV system in a more principled way.

$$
h'(t) = A(t)h(t) + B(t)x(t)
$$

The general intuition of our framework is that if one has some regular ODE without the $B$ term, i.e., $f'(t) = Af(t)$, this has a closed form solution $f(t) = e^{(t - t_0)A} f(t_0)$.
The one-step discrete update is then $x_{t+1} = e^{\Delta A}x_t$
Here, since the derivative includes $Ah(t)$, the state directly impacts the rate of change. Thus, the parameterization of A can rapidly adjust the dynamics of the system, which forces explicit methods, like Euler, to take small $\Delta$ steps.
To mitigate this, one would use an integrating factor of $e^{-At}$ to directly address the exponential dynamics.
If this doesn't make too much sense, hopefully seeing how it applies to our system will help.


Taking our $h\'(t)=A(t)h(t)+B(t)x(t)$ system, we apply a discretization factor of $e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}$ as the $A$ is now time-varying.

$$
\begin{aligned}
e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}h'(t) &= e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}A(t)h(t) + e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}B(t)x(t) \\
(e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}h(t))' &= e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}B(t)x(t) 
\end{aligned}
$$

since $(e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds})\' = \int_0^t -A(s)ds e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds} = -A(t)e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}$.

Thus, when we want to discretize between timesteps $[\tau_{t-1}, \tau_t]$, we can just integrate both sides between the range. For ease of notation, I'm going represent $z(t) = e^{\int_0^t -A(s)ds}$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tfrac{d}{dt}(z(t)h(t)) &= z(t)B(t)x(t) \\
\int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t}\tfrac{d}{d\tau}z(\tau)h(\tau)d\tau &= \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t}z(\tau)B(\tau)x(\tau)d\tau \\
z(\tau_{t})h(\tau_{t}) - z(\tau_{t-1})h(\tau_{t-1}) &= \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t}z(\tau)B(\tau)x(\tau)d\tau \\
z(\tau_{t})h(\tau_{t}) &= z(\tau_{t-1})h(\tau_{t-1}) + \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t}z(\tau)B(\tau)x(\tau)d\tau \\
\end{aligned}
$$

Rearranging into a more familiar form and substituting back the $z(\tau)$ value:

$$
\begin{aligned}
h(\tau_{t}) &= z(\tau_{t})^{-1}z(\tau_{t-1})h(\tau_{t-1}) + z(\tau_{t})^{-1}\int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t}z(\tau)B(\tau)x(\tau)d\tau \\
h(\tau_t) &= \exp\left(\int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t}A(s)ds\right)h(\tau_{t-1}) + \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(\int_{\tau}^{\tau_t}A(s)ds\right)B(\tau)x(\tau) d\tau
\end{aligned}
$$

Now, we've solved state update analytically through our integration factor.
This means the most "difficult" part of the system can be directly calculated, and the remaining integral can be approximated.

Because $A(s)$ is continuous, we "sample" it with a right-hold assumption where $\forall s \in [\tau_{t-1},\tau_t], A(s) = A(\tau_t)$, resulting in

$$
h_t \approx \exp(\Delta_t A_t)h_{t-1} + \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(\int_{\tau}^{\tau_t}A(s)ds\right)B(\tau)x(\tau)d\tau
$$

{% details When can the transition integral be directly calculated? %}
If $A$ is LTI, then the state transition integral is exactly $\exp(A \Delta_t)$.
{% enddetails %}

This final equation lays the foundation for deriving the prior Mamba discretization methods and is also the inspiration of our "Exponential-X" style name.
The application of the integration constant can be seen as a style of exponential tilting, and X is a stand-in for the various ways to approximate the remaining integral.

### Recovering Prior Mamba Discretization
As previously mentioned, prior Mamba discretizations differ in theory and implementation.
But with our new discretization derivation, we can recover both the theoretical ZOH discretization and implemented Exponential-Euler discretization.
We have already recovered the $\bar{A}$ term for both, so we will focus only on the remaining integral.

**ZOH**: Assuming similar assumptions where $B(\tau), x(\tau)$ are constant and sampled at the right endpoint, 
$$
\begin{aligned}
% \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(\int_{\tau}^{\tau_t}A(s)ds\right)B(\tau)x(\tau)d\tau &= B(\tau_t)x(\tau_t) \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(A(\tau_t)\int_{\tau}^{\tau_t}ds\right)d\tau \\
(\cdot) &= B(\tau_t)x(\tau_t) \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(A_t\int_{\tau}^{\tau_t}ds\right)d\tau \\
&= B_t\,x_t\exp(A_t\tau_t) \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(-A_t\tau\right)d\tau \\
&= B_t\,x_t\exp(A_t\tau_t)\dfrac{1}{A_t}\left(\exp(-A_t\tau_{t-1}) - \exp(-A_t\tau_t)\right) \\
&= A_t^{-1}\left(\exp(A_t(\tau_t - \tau_{t-1})) - I\right)B_t\,x_t \\
&= A_t^{-1}\left(\exp({\Delta_tA_t})-I\right)B_t\,x_t
\end{aligned}
$$

**Exponential-Euler**: Once again, we approximate the integral with Euler's rule and hold the $B, x$ terms to the right endpoint.
$$
\begin{aligned}
(\cdot) &= B(\tau_t)x(\tau_t) \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(A(\tau_t)\int_{\tau}^{\tau_t}ds\right)d\tau \\
&= B_t\,x_t\exp(A_t\tau_t) \int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(-A_t\tau\right)d\tau \\
&\approx B_t\,x_t\exp(A_t\tau_t) (\tau_t - \tau_{t-1})\exp\left(-A_t\tau_t\right) \\
&= \Delta_t\,B_t\,x_t \\
\end{aligned}
$$

### New Exponential-Trapezoidal Discretization
Thus, the linchpin of turning continuous-time SSMs into tangible recurrences is how to approximate $\int_{\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t} \exp\left(-A_t\tau\right)d\tau$.
We've shown above that one can either analytically solve it (ZOH) or approximate it (Euler), but what if we don't want to use inverses but want something more accurate than Euler's?

We can use the trapezoid method to approximate the integral.
Instead of the standard method which takes the average of both endpoints, we use a convex combination which we find empirically performs better.
The integral then evaluates to

$$\Delta_t \left(\lambda_t \exp(-A_t \tau_t) + (1 - \lambda_t) \exp(-A_t \tau_{t-1})\right)$$

and once plugging into our overall discretization method results in 

$$
\begin{aligned} 
& B_t\,x_t\exp(A_t\tau_t) \Delta_t \left(\lambda_t \exp(-A_t \tau_t) + (1 - \lambda_t) \exp(-A_t \tau_{t-1})\right) \\
=& (1-\lambda_t)\Delta_t\,\exp({\Delta_tA_t})\,B_{t-1}\,x_{t-1} + \lambda_t\,\Delta_t\,B_t\,x_t
\end{aligned}
$$

Interesting, our new Exponential-Trapezoid based recurrence implicitly applies a convolution on the hidden state $h$.
Let's rewrite our recurrence so this doesn't get *too* messy:

$$h_t = \alpha_t h_{t-1} + \beta_t B_{t-1}x_{t-1} + \gamma_t B_tx_t$$

where $\alpha_t = e^{\Delta_t A_t}, \beta_t=(1-\gamma_t)e^{\Delta_t A_t},\gamma_t=\gamma_t\Delta_t$.

If we expand the recurrence where $h_{-1}=0$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
h_0 & = \gamma_0 B_0x_0 \\
h_1 & = (\alpha_1 \gamma_0 + \beta_1)B_0x_0 + \gamma_1 B_1x_1 \\
h_2 & = \alpha_2(\alpha_1 \gamma_0 + \beta_1)B_0x_0 + (\alpha_2 \gamma_1 + \beta_2)B_1x_1 + \gamma_2 B_2x_2 \\
... \\
h_T & = \alpha_{T\dots2}(\alpha_1 \gamma_0 + \beta_1)B_0x_0 + \ldots + \gamma_T B_Tx_T 
\end{aligned}
$$

, we can express the output as a matrix operation

$$
\small
\begin{bmatrix}
y_0 \\ y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots
\end{bmatrix} = 
\left(
\begin{bmatrix}
    \gamma_0 & & \\
    (\gamma_0\alpha_1 + \beta_1) & \gamma_1 & \\
    \alpha_2(\gamma_0\alpha_1 + \beta_1) & (\gamma_1\alpha_2+\beta_2) & \gamma_2 \\
    \vdots & & & \ddots \\
\end{bmatrix} \odot
\begin{bmatrix}
    C_0^\top B_0 & & & \\
    C_1^\top B_0 & C_1^\top B_1 & & \\
    C_2^\top B_0 & C_2^\top B_1 & C_2^\top B_2 & \\
    \vdots & & & \ddots \\
\end{bmatrix}
\right)
\begin{bmatrix}
    x_0 \\ x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots
\end{bmatrix} 
$$

Now to show that our recurrence actually applies a convolution on the state input, we need to first decompose the coefficient matrix

$$
\small
\begin{aligned}
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}
    \gamma_0 & & & \\
    (\gamma_0\alpha_1 + \beta_1) & \gamma_1 & & \\
    \alpha_2(\gamma_0\alpha_1 + \beta_1) & (\gamma_1\alpha_2+\beta_2) & \gamma_2 & \\
    \vdots & & & \ddots \\
\end{bmatrix}}_{L}
=
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}
1      &        &        &  \\
\alpha_1 &    1    &        &  \\
\alpha_2\alpha_1 & \alpha_2       & 1      &  \\
\vdots  &        & & \ddots \\
\end{bmatrix}}_{L_1} \;
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}
\gamma_0 &        &        &  \\
\beta_1  & \gamma_1       &        &  \\
0        & \beta_2       & \gamma_2 &  \\
\vdots   &        &  & \ddots \\
\end{bmatrix}}_{L_2}
\end{aligned}
$$

and then use some tensor contractions. The original operation can be denoted as 

$$
Y = \mathsf{contract}(\mathtt{TN,SN,TS,SP} \to \mathtt{TP})(C,B,L,X)
$$

which can be expanded to

$$
Y = \mathsf{contract}(\mathtt{TN,SN,TJ,JS,SP} \to \mathtt{TP})(C,B,L_1,L_2,X)
$$

given our coefficient matrix decomposition. The final output can then be divided into a three step process

$$
  \begin{aligned}
    Z &= \mathsf{contract}(\mathtt{SN,SP,JS} \to \mathtt{JNP})(B, X, L_2) \\
    H &= \mathsf{contract}(\mathtt{TJ,JNP} \to \mathtt{TNP})(L_1,Z) \\
    Y &= \mathsf{contract}(\mathtt{TN,TNP} \to \mathtt{TP})(C,Z)
  \end{aligned}
$$

where $Z$ can be seen as the result of applying a structured two-conv on the $Bx$ outer product prior to calculating the state $H$.

> This process is similar to what Mamba-2's SSD also did. Although we didn't cover SSD in our Mamba recap, our earlier Mamba-2 [blogs]({% post_url 2024-05-31-mamba2-part2-theory %}) do a very nice job detailing the theory behind it.

## Complex-valued SSM

Recently, there has been an influx of work that focus on determining the expressivity of linear RNN-style models <d-cite key='grazzi2025unlockingstatetrackinglinearrnns'></d-cite><d-cite key='merrill2025illusionstatestatespacemodels'></d-cite><d-cite key='cirone2025theoreticalfoundationsdeepselective'></d-cite><d-cite key='Hahn_2024'></d-cite>.
Notably, these papers find that despite the "state" found within such linear mechanisms, they are fundamentally constrained due to the lack of the non-linearity transition between timesteps.

While more complex state transitions, such as diagonal plus low-rank (DPLR) in older LTI SSM models, can improve the method's expressivity, the simplification of transitions from diagonal to scalar times identity across iterations of LTV SSMs has resulted in simplest state-tracking tasks being out-of-reach for Mamba style models.
The inability to solve some of these synthetics may signal poor performance in practice where models might need to keep track of parenthesis and diffs for coding or actions and states throughout a sequence.

### What's Parity?

For instance, parity, determining whether the sum of a sequence of 0's and 1's is even, is unsolvable by Mamba models in a constant number of layers.
The general intuition of the ideal solution is that the hidden state keeps track of the "even-ness"/"odd-ness" up to the current timestep with a two-state automaton that flips between even and odd depending on the next input.
While this seems simple enough, current Mamba models constrain the transition $\bar{A}_t \in [0,1]$, which forces the model to learn the naive solution which is to add all the values together then mod 2.
This does work for shorter sequences but quickly becomes infeasible when the sequence becomes longer than what the state can represent.

But, parity and other modulo tasks can be solved with rotations!
The way one can visualize how rotations solve modulo $m$ problems is that one has some 2D vector that can be rotated around the origin.
The entire possible angle distribution $[0, 2\pi]$ is partitioned into $m$ sections, and the vector is rotated by $\tfrac{2\pi}{m}$ to align with the current running modulo remainder.

### Representing with Real-valued SSM

Working with complex values in computer systems is quite a pain due to their multiplicative interactions. Luckily for us, diagonal complex-valued continuous SSM *can* be represented as discretized real-valued SSMs (without any additional approximation loss compared to standard discretization).
While the full proof can found within our paper, the general intuition is to expand the original $N$-sized state complex-valued SSM to a $2N$-sized real-valued where each complex-valued dimension is split into its real and imaginary counterpart, and the transition matrix is partitioned into its scaling and oscillatory portions.
With the commutative property the scaling and oscillatory components in effect (due to the diagonal structure of the underlying matrix), we can map the continuous diagonal complex transition into a block-diagonal, scaled rotation transformation.

{% details Converting general complex SSMs to real-valued SSMs  %}
It is also possible to convert a general, unstructured complex SSM transition matrix, i.e., not diagonal, etc., to a real-valued SSM, though the scaled rotation intuition breaks down.
The conversion still doubles the state size and while the expansion of the $B, C, x$ will remain similar, the transition matrix will no longer be as simple.
With an unstructured transition matrix $\mathbf{A} + i\Theta$, the exponential (resulting from our integrating factor technique) cannot be factored as 

$$\exp(\Delta(\mathbf{A} + i\Theta)) \neq \exp(\Delta\mathbf{A}) \exp(i\Delta\Theta)$$

since $\mathbf{A},\Theta$ generally do not commute, unlike the diagonal case.
Consequently, while a real-valued equivalent exists, computing it would require the expensive full matrix exponential.
{% enddetails %}

Eventually, under the prior Exponential-Euler discretization, we obtain the following recurrence 

$$
\begin{aligned}
h_t &= e^{\Delta_t A_t}
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos(\Delta_t \theta_t) & -\sin(\Delta_t \theta_t) \\
\sin(\Delta_t \theta_t) & \cos(\Delta_t \theta_t)
\end{bmatrix}}_{R_t} h_{t-1} + \Delta_t B_t x_t
\end{aligned}
$$

for a $N=2$ state. For larger states, the rotation matrix $R_t$ is block-diagonal and the $\theta$'s can differ.

### Efficient Implementation with RoPE Trick

Great, now we've shown that we can implement a complex SSM without having to explicitly model the imaginary components!
But another issue remains: the rotation of the hidden state requires us to reimplement the kernels to incorporate this new type of transition --- more moving parts, rotating the entire hidden state, etc. --- seems like quite the hassle *ugh*... or is it?

Luckily for us, given the structure of $A$, we can sidestep all of this and directly adjust the $B, C$ to achieve the same goal.
This is because the output for timestep $t$, the output can be modeled as 

$$y_t = C^\top_t\bar{B}_t + \cdots + C^\top_t(\bar{A}R)^\times_{t\cdots 1}\bar{B}_0$$

Under the assumption that $\bar{A}$ is scalar times identity (it also works if $\bar{A}$ is diagonal but every two values are shared), we can ignore the $\bar{A}$ terms for now by absorbing it into $C$.
This results in the term $C_i^\top R_i \cdots R_{j+1} \bar{B}_j$ which can be represented by $\left(R_i \cdots R_0 C_i\right)^\top \left(R_j \cdots R_0 \bar{B}_j\right)$.
The $\bar{A}$ terms can be added back at this point.
Thus, it's apparent that the rotations can be embedded into the $B, C$ terms prior to passing into the original kernels.

> The application of our data-dependent rotations onto $B, C$ can be done efficiently. Instead of performing numerous matrix multiplications, we can run a cumsum over the $\theta$'s and perform the efficient realization of rotary matrix multiplication as covered in the RoFormer paper <d-cite key='su2023roformerenhancedtransformerrotary'></d-cite>, which itself used data-independent rotations. This inspired us to call the use of a vanilla SSM to compute a complex SSM as the "RoPE trick."

The RoPE trick extends to our Exponential-Trapezoidal recurrence, and we empirically validate that our complex SSM is able to solve state-tracking tasks previously too hard for prior Mambas.

## Multi-Input, Multi-Output

The compute paradigm in scaling LLMs has shifted from training and inference in the past year or so.
Nowadays, more and more compute is dedicated to the actual deployment and usage of these models.
To some degree, the writing was on the wall; chain-of-thought and in-context learning techniques dramatically improved performance just by making the models think longer and process more.
With the advent of agentic workflows, we have Claude agents spawning Claude subagents and so forth.

### Working while Memory-Bound

Compared to the compute-bound nature of training, the deployment of the same models, especially their decoding process, are memory-bound.
Throughout training, the hardware is constantly performing operations, but during decoding, the compute units of the hardware sit idle for large swathes of time as it waits for data to be moved across different levels of memory hierarchy.
As an example of why this happens, think about a simple MLP.
During training, the entire sequence is processed, but during decode, only the current token is processed as the past tokens are cached.
The latency spent moving the MLP weights are around the same for both training and decode, but can be amortized a lot better over more computation under a training regime.

So with current linear models where the state update and output calculation can be performed in constant time, compute sits idle for most of the time.
One way to estimate how "hard" the hardware is working is through arithmetic intensity, a ratio of compute performed to memory moved. Given $P$ SSM of state size $N$, the arithmetic intensity is around 2 ($\tfrac{\textsf{compute}}{\textsf{memory}} = \tfrac{P (5N)}{2 (1 + 2N + P + NP)} \approx 2.5$) given two-byte values.
To put this into context, an arithmetic intensity of around 300 is what the threshold between memory-bound and compute-bound is... *yikes*

Since we pay for the entire rack, how can we use as much of it as possible?

### The MIMO System
{% details Q: How does one increase a ratio? %}
A: Either increase the numerator or decrease the denominator.
{% enddetails %}

In the case of SSMs, we've seen empirically that the state size is quite important for performance... so, let's keep that the same. 
Now how do we increase the compute required for calculating the hidden state recurrence?
We can make the current (scalar) product of $\bar{B}_t$ and $x_t$ to be a dot product by expanding the dimension of $\bar{B}_t$ to $N \times r$ and $x_t$ to $r$ where $r$ is the rank of the system.
The expansion of $x_t$ from a scalar to a vector is also applied to the output $y_t$ by converting $C_t$ from a $N$ vector to a $N \times r$ matrix.
These changes increase the arithmetic intensity of the new SSM to $2r$ as opposed to $2.5$.

Referring back to our state space/control theory toolbox, this is equivalent to converting the regular SISO (single-input, single-output) SSM to multi-input, multi-output (MIMO).

The final recurrence is

$$
\begin{aligned}
h_{t} &= A_t h_{t-1} + \mathcal{B}_t \mathbf{x}_t \\
\mathbf{y}_t &= \mathcal{C}_t^\top h_t
\end{aligned}
$$

where $\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{(N\times r)}, \mathbf{x, y}\in\mathbb{R}^r$ and the hidden state remains $\mathbb{R}^N$.

The arithmetic intensity during decode increases as more compute is required while maintaining the same hidden state size.

**The increased inference costs can be "hidden" due to the memory bottleneck, but training, which is already compute-bound, increases linearly.**

### Intuition and Parameterization

The MIMO SSM can be interpreted as running $r$ SISO in parallel with a shared transition $A_t$ across them all.
This means that all the improvements introduced for vanilla SSMs, like our exponential-trapezoidal discretization and complex-valued SSM, can be directly applied to MIMO ones.
Since the state size is the same between a SISO and MIMO SSM, we can convert all SSMs within an architecture to MIMO to increase expressivity without sacrificing decode speed.

However, the conversion must be done carefully without scaling the number of parameters by a factor of $r$.
Due to Mamba models' multi-value attention (MVA) structure, we can directly parameterize the projection that maps the input into $\mathcal{B,C}$ but the $x$ term is a bit tricky.
Instead of directly projecting $u_t \to \mathbf{x}_t$, we maintain the original projection from prior Mambas which results in $x_t$.
The expansion of $x_t \to \mathbf{x}_t$ is done through a learnable $r$ vector.
When taking into account all $P$ SSMs, we reduce the parameter count from $P^2r$ to $P^2 + Pr$, which is quite the reduction!

We show that our MIMO variant of Mamba-3 further improves the already strong performance of regular Mamba-3 at all scales we tested on.

> We want to underscore that MIMO will result in an $r$ linear increase in training (and potentially prefill) time due to its compute-bound nature. The memory-bound decoding phase hides the increased cost and results in little change in latency.
